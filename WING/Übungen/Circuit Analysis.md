Applied to **Node A** (the top junction), this yields: | $$\sum I_{\text{in}} = \sum I_{\text{out}} \implies I_1 + I_g = I_2$$ | _(Where $I_2$ is the current flowing down through $R_2$)_ |

|**How was it determined that $U_g$ is a voltage rise (positive term)?**|A voltage source is counted as a **rise (positive term)** when the chosen traversal path crosses it **from the negative terminal to the positive terminal**. The $U_g$ arrow defined the polarity, and the clockwise traversal crossed from the tail $(-) \to$ head $(+)$.|
|**What is the sign convention for $R_2$?**|Resistors are **loads**, and voltage is counted as a **drop (negative term)** when the traversal path is in the direction of the current flow ($I_2$). Since $I_2$ flows down, setting the top as $(+)$ and the bottom as $(-)$, the clockwise traversal crossed from $(+) \to (-)$, resulting in the term **$-U_2$**.|
|**$U_2$ points down, but I thought direction of voltage points to a positive terminal?**|This clarifies the **Passive Sign Convention**. For a **load** like a resistor, the voltage arrow ($U_2$) points from the $(+)$ terminal to the $(-)$ terminal to signify a **voltage drop** (energy being dissipated) in the direction of the current. This is standard practice for components that consume energy.|
