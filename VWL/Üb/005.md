### 1. Mathematical Formulation of Price Elasticity
* **Point Elasticity Operator:** The price elasticity of demand ($\epsilon$) operationalizes the marginal percentage change in quantity demanded relative to a marginal percentage change in price.
  $$\epsilon = \frac{\% \Delta Q}{\% \Delta P} = \frac{\partial Q}{\partial P} \cdot \frac{P}{Q}$$
* **Geometric Decomposition:** The formula structurally decouples into two mathematically distinct components:
  1. $\frac{\partial Q}{\partial P}$: The mathematical slope of the demand function (the inverse derivative of the inverse demand function), which is structurally negative for ordinary goods due to the Law of Demand.
  2. $\frac{P}{Q}$: The coordinates of the specific price-quantity vector on the demand trajectory where elasticity is evaluated.

---

### 2. Quantitative Classification & Interpretation Framework
* **The Absolute Value Metric:** Because $\epsilon \le 0$ under standard economic assumptions, microeconomic interpretation relies strictly on the magnitude of its absolute value ($|\epsilon|$).
* **Structural Categories:**
  * **Elastische Nachfrage ($| \epsilon | > 1$):** The percentage change in quantity demanded dominates the percentage change in price. A $1\%$ upward price adjustment induces an over-proportional ($> 1\%$) contraction in volume demanded. Total revenue drops when prices rise in this zone.
  * **Unelastische Nachfrage ($| \epsilon | < 1$):** The percentage change in quantity demanded is compressed relative to the price adjustment. A $1\%$ price increase causes an under-proportional ($< 1\%$) drop in volume demanded. Total revenue expands when prices rise in this zone.
  * **Isoelastische / Einheitselastische Nachfrage ($| \epsilon | = 1$):** Percentage changes in price and quantity clear perfectly symmetrically. Total revenue is maximized at this exact inflection point.

### 3. Theoretical Determinants of Price Elasticity
* **Availability of Substitutes (Substitutionsgüter):** The single most critical structural driver. If a commodity has close, readily available substitutes, consumers shift demand instantly away from price-increased goods, resulting in highly elastic profiles ($|\epsilon| > 1$). Absence of replacements (e.g., insulin) binds consumers to the good, creating extreme inelasticity ($|\epsilon| < 1$).
* **Temporal Horizon (Zeithorizont):** Demand elasticity is a monotonic function of time. In the short run, consumer behavior is locked by structural friction, lack of information, or fixed physical assets (inelastic). Over a long-run horizon, consumers adjust consumption habits, swap operational capital (e.g., trading a combustion vehicle for an electric car), and unlock structural flexibility (elastic).
* **Market Definition Geometry:** The elasticity profile depends heavily on how broadly the relevant market boundary is specified. Narrow definitions (e.g., a specific brand of vanilla ice cream) feature extreme elasticity because alternate brands act as perfect substitutes. Broad definitions (e.g., food or dairy as a macroeconomic aggregate) have zero viable substitutes, locking consumers into highly inelastic demand patterns.
* **Budget Share Allocation (Anteil am Budget):** High-value expenditures that absorb a significant fraction of a consumer's nominal income demand meticulous optimization, rendering them highly sensitive to price fluctuations (elastic). Micro-expenditures (e.g., table salt) represent an unnoticeable share of aggregate expenditure, meaning price shifts go largely unheeded by the consumer (inelastic).
