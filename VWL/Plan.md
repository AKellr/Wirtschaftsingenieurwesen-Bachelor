### I. Konsumententheorie (Household & Consumer Theory)

Your exam tests the core mechanics of consumer choice, specifically calculating the optimal bundle of goods and classifying goods based on demand changes.

**Priority Concepts to Learn:**

- **Utility Maximization:** The mathematical derivation of the household optimum given a budget constraint.
    
- **Elasticities:** Preiselastizität (PED), Kreuzpreiselastizität (XED), and Einkommenselastizität. You must know how to interpret these values to classify goods (e.g., normal, inferior, substitutes).
    

**Specific Questions to Practice:**

- **Analytical Household Optimum (Aufgabe 6):** Practice setting up the Lagrange function $\mathcal{L} = U(x_1,x_2) + \lambda(I - p_1x_1 - p_2x_2)$. You must be completely fluent in calculating the partial derivatives, setting them to zero, and determining the marginal rate of substitution (GRS) to find optimal quantities.
    
- **Elasticity Calculation (Aufgabe 8):** Practice calculating elasticities using the formula $\epsilon = \frac{\partial q}{\partial I} \frac{I}{q}$ or $\epsilon = \frac{\partial q}{\partial p} \frac{p}{q}$. Ensure you can immediately state the rule: if $\epsilon > 0$, it is a normal/superior good; if $\epsilon < 0$, it is an inferior good.
    
- **Graphical Representation (Aufgabe 7):** Practice drawing the budget line (Budgetgerade) and indifference curves (Indifferenzkurven). Be able to accurately mark the tangential point where the slope of the indifference curve equals the relative prices of the goods.
    

### II. Produzententheorie (Producer Theory)

The focus here is on how firms minimize costs for a given output or maximize output for a given cost.

**Priority Concepts to Learn:**

- **Production Functions:** Cobb-Douglas functions and the concept of marginal products.
    
- **Cost Minimization:** The producer equivalent of the household optimum, finding the optimal combination of capital and labor.
    

**Specific Questions to Practice:**

- **The Expansion Path (Aufgabe 9):** Practice determining the cost-minimizing input combination using Lagrange: $\mathcal{L} = wL + rK + \lambda(\bar{q} - f(K,L))$. You must be able to solve this to find the ratio of factors (e.g., $r_2 = f(r_1)$) which represents the expansion path (Expansionspfad).
    
- **Cost Functions:** Ensure you can derive Marginal Costs (MC) and Average Total Costs (ATC) from a given total cost function, as this is required for the supply curve and market equilibrium calculations.
    

### III. Marktgleichgewicht, Marktformen & Wohlfahrt (Market Equilibrium & Structures)

This is the most heavily weighted section of your exam, spanning five separate questions (Aufgaben 1-5). It requires you to contrast perfect competition (Polypol) with monopolies and oligopolies.

**Priority Concepts to Learn:**

- **Profit Maximization Conditions:** For perfect competition, $p = MC$. For monopolies, $MR = MC$ (Grenzumsatz = Grenzkosten).
    
- **Welfare Analysis:** Calculating Consumer Surplus (Konsumentenrente) and Producer Surplus (Produzentenrente) using integrals or triangle area formulas.
    

**Specific Questions to Practice:**

- **Polypol Analysis (Aufgabe 4 & 5):** Practice setting aggregate demand equal to aggregate supply to find the equilibrium price and quantity ($q_D = q_S$). Calculate the exact numerical values for consumer and producer surplus and practice graphing the supply and demand curves, carefully shading the surplus areas.
    
- **Monopoly Analysis (Aufgabe 2 & 3):** Practice calculating the Cournot point (Cournot'scher Punkt). This requires taking the inverse demand function $p(x)$, calculating the revenue function $R(x) = p(x) \cdot x$, finding the marginal revenue $R'(x)$, and setting it equal to marginal cost $K'(x)$. Practice calculating the resulting deadweight loss (Wohlfahrtsverlust) and graphing the distinct monopoly pricing structure.
    
- **Oligopoly/Duopoly (Aufgabe 1):** Practice Stackelberg and Cournot competition models. The scenario where "ein Anbieter dem zweiten die Mengenentscheidung diktieren kann" specifically refers to the Stackelberg leader-follower model. You need to practice deriving the reaction function of the follower and substituting it into the leader's profit maximization problem.
    

### IV. Marktversagen & Öffentliche Güter (Market Failure & Public Goods)

This section tests the limits of the free market, specifically dealing with goods that are non-excludable and non-rivalrous.

**Priority Concepts to Learn:**

- **Public Goods:** Characteristics of public goods and the free-rider problem (Trittbrettfahrer-Problem).
    
- **Externalities:** Internalization of costs and the Coase-Theorem.
    

**Specific Questions to Practice:**

- **Strategic Decision Making (Aufgabe 10):** Practice evaluating payoff matrices and cost functions for public goods. You must be able to calculate the critical mass (Kritische Koalitionsgröße) required for a project to be viable by comparing the net utility of participating versus defecting.

### 1. Market Equilibrium, Interventions & Welfare (38% of the Exam)

This is the absolute core of the exam, combining _Aufgabe 5_ and _Aufgabe 6_ for a total of 40 points.

- You must be able to calculate the unmanaged market equilibrium by setting the supply function equal to the demand function to find $p^*$ and $q^*$.
    
- You need to confidently calculate Consumer Surplus (CS) and Producer Surplus (PS) using the areas of the triangles.
    
- You must calculate the exact shifts when the government introduces a quantity tax ($t$), including the new equilibrium quantity, the gross price for consumers, and the net price for producers.
    
- You are required to calculate the Government Revenue ($G$) and the resulting Deadweight Loss (DWL) from taxation.
    
- You must be able to graphically sketch supply and demand functions, properly labeling the axes, the price ceilings (Höchstpreis), the resulting market shortage (Nachfrageüberhang), and the areas representing welfare shifts.
    

### 2. Constrained Optimization: Consumer & Producer Theory (31% of the Exam)

This covers _Aufgabe 2_ and _Aufgabe 4_, totaling 32 points, and relies entirely on formal calculus.

- For Consumer Theory, you must maximize a given utility function (often a Cobb-Douglas format like $U = q_1^2 q_2$) subject to a strict budget constraint.
    
- For Producer Theory, you must minimize costs given a specific production quantity constraint and input prices for Labor ($L$) and Capital ($K$).
    
- In both scenarios, you must formally set up the Lagrangian function ($\mathcal{L}$), calculate the partial derivatives, and solve the system of equations to find the optimal quantities and the Lagrange multiplier ($\lambda$).
    
- You must also evaluate a production function to determine its Returns to Scale (Skalenerträge) and interpret what that means economically.
    

### 3. International Trade (14% of the Exam)

This is tested in _Aufgabe 7_ for 15 points and is purely graphical and conceptual.

- You must be able to graph a domestic market open to free trade, specifically where the world price ($p_W$) is lower than the closed domestic equilibrium.
    
- You need to visually map out Consumer and Producer Surplus in this open economy.
    
- You must graphically demonstrate the effects of a government introducing an import tariff ($t$) and explain why a tariff is generally preferred over an import quota regarding domestic welfare.
    

### 4. Fundamentals & Price Elasticity (16% of the Exam)

These are quick, high-value tasks found in _Aufgabe 1_ and _Aufgabe 3_.

- You must know the formula for the price elasticity of demand ($\epsilon$) and calculate it for a specific price point.
    
- You need to interpret the elasticity result (e.g., recognizing that an absolute value greater than 1 means demand is elastic) and list its theoretical determinants.
    
- You must know how to calculate both accounting profit (Buchhalterischer Gewinn) and economic profit, understanding that the latter subtracts implicit opportunity costs.
    
- You need to memorize definitions for core concepts like "Homo Oeconomicus," "Allmendegüter" (Tragedy of the Commons), and the difference between short-run and long-run costs.
