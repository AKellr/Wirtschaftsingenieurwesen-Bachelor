### 1. Market Equilibrium in a Closed vs. Open Economy
* **Autarky (Closed Market):** The domestic market equilibrium is determined strictly by the intersection of the downward-sloping domestic demand curve and the upward-sloping domestic supply curve. Price ($P_{A}$) and quantity ($Q_{A}$) are regulated purely by domestic market clearance.
* **Free Trade Framework:** Opening the economy introduces a perfectly elastic world supply curve at the world price ($p_W$). 
* **Price Dynamics:** Per the constraints in `image_b66dbb.png`, the world price is lower than the closed domestic equilibrium ($p_W < P_{A}$). Because consumers have unrestricted access to international markets, the domestic price drops immediately to $p_W$.
* **Structural Shortage & Imports:** At $p_W$, domestic quantity demanded expands significantly while domestic quantity supplied contracts. This operational gap between domestic demand and domestic supply is balanced entirely via international imports ($M_{FT}$).

---

### 2. Operational Mechanics of an Import Tariff ($t$)
* **Price Alteration:** The introduction of a specific import tariff ($t$) acts as a tax on foreign goods, shifting the effective domestic price level upward from $p_W$ to $p_W + t$.
* **Behavioral Responses:**
  * **Domestic Consumers:** The higher price induces a contraction along the demand curve, lowering the total quantity demanded.
  * **Domestic Producers:** The price increase yields higher marginal returns, causing an expansion along the supply curve and increasing domestic production.
  * **Import Volume:** The dual movement of expanding domestic supply and contracting domestic demand structurally shrinks the domestic market gap, reducing net import volume.

---

### 3. Welfare Allocation & Surplus Mapping
* **Consumer Surplus (CS):** Decreases significantly. Consumers suffer a welfare loss because they face higher unit prices ($p_W + t$) and reduce their total consumption volume.
* **Producer Surplus (PS):** Increases. Domestic firms capture a welfare gain (protectionist rent) because they sell a larger quantity at a higher domestic market price.
* **Government Revenue:** The state generates direct fiscal revenue calculated as the tariff rate multiplied by the new import volume: 
  $$\text{Tariff Revenue} = t \times M_{\text{Tariff}}$$
* **Deadweight Loss (DWL):** The tariff introduces two distinct areas of economic waste: a production distortion (inefficient domestic firms replacing lower-cost foreign producers) and a consumption distortion (lost utility due to discouraged consumption).

---

### 4. Comparative Welfare Analysis: Tariff vs. Quota
* **Equivalence in Price/Quantity:** An import quota (quantitative restriction) can be calibrated to restrict imports to the exact same volume as a tariff, leading to an identical domestic price ($p_W + t$), identical consumer surplus loss, and identical producer surplus gain.
* **The Revenue Divergence (Quotenrente):**
  * **Tariff Mechanism:** The economic premium generated by the price wedge ($t$) is collected directly by the domestic government as public revenue, remaining within the domestic welfare calculus.
  * **Quota Mechanism:** The price wedge generates a scarcity premium known as **Quota Rent (Quotenrente)**. If the government distributes import licenses without a competitive bidding process (e.g., allocating them directly to foreign exporters or specific trading entities), license holders buy at $p_W$ and resell at the artificially inflated domestic price.
* **Welfare Optimality:** A tariff is strictly preferred over an import quota regarding domestic welfare because the quota rent frequently escapes the domestic economy (flowing abroad). A tariff guarantees this financial premium is retained domestically as government revenue to benefit national public welfare.
